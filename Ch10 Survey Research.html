The Logic of Survey Research

Survey research is the most widely used method in social science. It involves asking a sample of people questions to learn about their attitudes, behaviors, and beliefs, and then using those answers to paint a picture of the larger population.

The Heart of the Survey

The questionnaire is the central instrument of any survey. Crafting good questions is an art that requires avoiding common pitfalls. Click each item to reveal more.

üö´ 12 Things to Avoid in Question Writing

+

1. Avoid Jargon & Slang: Use language your entire sample will understand.

2. Avoid Ambiguity: Be precise. "What's your income?" is vague. "What was your total pre-tax income in 2024?" is better.

3. Avoid Emotional Language: Words like "slaughter" or "freedom" can bias responses.

4. Avoid Prestige Bias: Don't link a statement to a prestigious person or group (e.g., "Do you agree with the President that...").

5. Avoid Double-Barreled Questions: Ask only one thing at a time. Don't ask, "Do you support the new tax and the new park?"

6. Avoid Leading Questions: Don't push respondents toward a certain answer (e.g., "You don't smoke, do you?").

7. Avoid Issues Beyond Respondent Capabilities: Don't ask questions people can't realistically answer.

8. Avoid False Premises: Don't start a question with a premise the respondent may disagree with.

9. Avoid Asking About Future Intentions: People are poor predictors of their own future behavior.

10. Avoid Double Negatives: They are confusing. "Do you disagree that students should not be required to..." is a bad question.

11. Avoid Overlapping Response Categories: Ensure answers are mutually exclusive (e.g., Age 18-25, 26-35, not 18-25, 25-35).

12. Avoid Unbalanced Response Categories: Offer an equal number of positive and negative choices.

‚ö†Ô∏è Social Desirability & Wording

+

People often give answers that make them look good, rather than what's truthful. This is the social desirability bias. For sensitive topics, careful, neutral wording is essential. Small changes in wording can produce huge differences in results.

Example: "Forbidding" speeches vs. "Not allowing" speeches gets different levels of support, even though they mean the same thing.

üß© Question Types & Layout

+
  • Open vs. Closed-Ended: Open-ended questions allow any answer, while closed-ended provide fixed choices.
  • Contingency Questions: Two-part questions where a second question's applicability depends on the answer to the first (e.g., "1. Have you ever smoked? If yes, 2. How often do you smoke?").
  • Matrix Questions: A grid format for efficiently asking about multiple items using the same response categories.
  • Order Effects: The order of questions matters. Early questions can prime respondents and influence their answers to later questions. Always start with easy, non-threatening questions.

The Art of the Interview

In telephone and face-to-face surveys, the interviewer is not just a question-reader; they are a critical part of the measurement process.

Build Rapport

The interviewer's first job is to gain trust and cooperation. A friendly, professional demeanor is key to getting honest answers.

Remain Neutral

The interviewer must read questions exactly as written and never show approval or disapproval of an answer. Their presence should not influence the responses.

Probe for Answers

When a respondent gives an incomplete or irrelevant answer, the interviewer uses neutral "probes" (e.g., "Could you tell me more about that?") to get a better response.
